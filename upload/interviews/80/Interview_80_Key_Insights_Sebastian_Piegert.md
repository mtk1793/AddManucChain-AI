## 1. CENTRALIZED VS. DECENTRALIZED: THE GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORK

**Insight:** Sebastian provided a precise framework for what MUST remain centralized vs. what CAN be distributed. This is the most actionable strategic guidance from any interviewee to date.

### What MUST Be Centralized (Non-Negotiable):

1. **Machine Calibration Standards**
   - All printing machines must operate identically
   - Calibration protocols must be identical
   - Maintenance standards must be identical
   - Sebastian: "You need to make sure that you will start with the basics and calibrate the machines against the same standards, maintain the machines against the same standards. That's absolutely non-negotiable."

2. **Powder/Feedstock Specifications**
   - Material composition must be standardized
   - Quality control on feedstock is non-negotiable
   - Traceability required (batch tracking)
   - Sebastian: "Off Powders and feedstock to the same specification absolutely"

3. **Process Parameters**
   - Temperature profiles must match
   - Cooling parameters must match
   - Post-print treatment protocols must match
   - Goal: "Get the same material out of the printer"

4. **Design Standards**
   - Part design must be centralized (OEM responsibility)
   - Performance specifications must be centralized
   - Certification requirements must be centralized
   - Critical for liability/warranty protection

### What CAN Be Decentralized (Flexible):

1. **Post-Processing Chain**
   - Can be in-house or outsourced
   - Can vary by location (different operational setups)
   - Less critical than printing itself
   - Sebastian: "What can be localized is really how you set up your post-processing chain. Then is it inside or outside of your company? So outsourcing a topic. So that's it's more freedom I guess."

2. **Powder Handling/Preparation**
   - How to mix, prepare, handle powder can vary
   - Must meet specifications, but methods can differ
   - Sebastian: "The powder itself that goes again and again into the process needs to fulfill certain requirements. But how you deal with mixing powder and whatnot that can be different in different sites."

3. **Operational Setup**
   - How facilities are organized locally
   - Staffing models (in-house vs. vendor)
   - Sebastian: "For example, something that you can adapt to the operational setup"

### Strategic Implication for AddManuChain:

This framework means **distributed manufacturing is possible IF you respect the hierarchy**:

**Tier 1 - Non-Negotiable (Must be controlled by you):**
- Machine specification/calibration protocols
- Feedstock sourcing/quality control
- Process parameter standardization
- Design control (you provide certified designs; partners execute)

**Tier 2 - Negotiable (Can be distributed to regional partners):**
- Post-processing methods
- Facility operations
- Local staffing/logistics
- Qualification/regulatory navigation

**Tier 3 - Partnership Model (Can be external vendors):**
- Partners source machines (to your spec)
- Partners source feedstock (to your spec)
- Partners execute processes (to your spec)
- Partners handle post-processing (methods can vary)
- Partners manage regulatory compliance (within your standards)

**Recommendation:** Position AddManuChain as "Tier 1 Controller + Tier 2/3 Enabler" - you provide standards and design; regional partners execute under your governance.

## 2. OEM BUSINESS MODEL: THREAT VS. OPPORTUNITY ANALYSIS

**Insight:** Sebastian was explicit about viewing third-party manufacturing as **primarily a threat**, not an opportunity. This is crucial for partnership strategy.

### Why OEMs See Distributed Manufacturing as Threat:

1. **Service Revenue Erosion**
   - Historically, OEMs earn significant margins on spare parts
   - If third parties can produce parts, customers become independent
   - Sebastian: "From our perspective as an OEM, we see this more as a threat. There an opportunity. Our customers that have engines in the fields, they probably see that more as an opportunity to be a bit more independent from us."

2. **Customer Control Loss**
   - OEMs currently control the maintenance relationship via parts scarcity
   - Third-party manufacturing allows customers to choose vendors
   - Reduces long-term contract leverage

3. **Quality/Warranty Risk**
   - If non-OEM parts fail, OEM may still bear liability
   - Quality control becomes indirect (can't inspect production)
   - Warranty claims become unpredictable
   - Sebastian: "It's simply a risk topic"

4. **Margin Cannibalization**
   - For "mature frames" (older turbines), third parties may undercut OEM pricing
   - Sebastian: "For for mature frames, we have this situation that there are so parties that offers capability. Now, so that's cannibalizing the market."

### Why OEMs MIGHT Accept It (Opportunity):

1. **Spare Parts Availability = Contract Driver**
   - Fast parts availability can command premium pricing
   - Sebastian: "If you come up with spare parts in no time. So that is a business case in itself."
   - If third parties accelerate availability, customers may accept higher service fees

2. **Supply Chain Resilience (Real Constraint)**
   - OEMs face physical scaling limits (can't expand internal capacity infinitely)
   - External vendors reduce risk of over-capacity investment
   - Sebastian: "External growth will not solve our next 4 years challenge, so we need to onboard external vendors for part production"

3. **New Design/Material Challenges**
   - Complex new designs (like hydrogen combustion) may require fresh expertise
   - Could be opportunity to find specialized partners
   - Sebastian: "But if you have your basics right then there is nothing that speaks against producing parts at more than one location"

### Strategic Implication:

**Be Clear About Your Role:**
- NOT: "We'll replace OEM supply chains"
- YES: "We'll handle overflow demand + obsolete parts + customer-preferred on-site production"

**Positioning Framework:**
- Frame as **demand fulfillment partner**, not competitor
- Handle:
  - Peak demand periods (OEM can't keep up)
  - Obsolete parts (OEM has discontinued)
  - On-site production (customer preference)
  - Aged fleet support (low-margin for OEM)
- Strictly NOT:
  - New, IP-critical designs
  - Parts under active warranty
  - Core customer relationships

## 5. CYBERSECURITY: THE UNDERESTIMATED CRITICAL PATH

**Insight:** Sebastian emphasized cybersecurity **multiple times** and was explicit about it being underestimated by potential partners: "Cybersecurity is a very important thing...So don't underestimate this issue about your business model and your business idea."

### Why Cybersecurity Matters:

1. **IP Protection Vehicle**
   - Shared digital platform = IP exposure risk
   - CAD designs, process parameters, materials specs all vulnerable
   - One compromised vendor = all IP exposed
   - Sebastian: "We usually do is we go through a due diligence with our external vendors regarding cybersecurity"

2. **Regulatory/Compliance Risk**
   - Some designs may be ITARS-controlled (export regulations)
   - Some processes may be classified (defence applications)
   - Cybersecurity breach = regulatory violation + legal liability

3. **Operational Continuity Risk**
   - If platform is hacked, all partner production could be disrupted
   - Ransomware vulnerability
   - Supply chain attack surface

### Current Practice at Siemens:

1. **Controlled Digital Spaces**
   - Separate collaboration platform for vendor access
   - Not an open API; controlled/gated access
   - Vendors can "pull information" but not freely browse
   - Sebastian: "We run a collaboration platform where you can put it's a controlled space, digital space, where you can put your models and all the specifications that you need to produce the part and then our vendor can pull the information from there."

2. **Due Diligence Process**
   - Formal cybersecurity assessment of each vendor
   - Standards must be maintained
   - Ongoing compliance monitoring

### Strategic Implication:

**Don't position as "open data platform."** Position as **"controlled governance platform with security standards."**

**Requirements for OEM Adoption:**
- Cybersecurity certification (ISO 27001 minimum; likely need SOC 2 Type II)
- Regular penetration testing / vulnerability assessments
- Vendor due diligence process (not just self-attestation)
- Data encryption (both transit and at rest)
- Access logging / audit trails
- Compliance with ITARS (if applicable)
- DND security clearance (if defence applications)

**Recommendation:**
- Build cybersecurity as **core platform feature**, not afterthought
- Get third-party security audit early (not after launch)
- Engage OEM compliance/security teams early in design
- Make this a competitive advantage, not compliance burden
