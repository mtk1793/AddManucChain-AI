## WHO HE IS & WHY HE MATTERS TO YOU

### The Person
Mohamad is a métallurgical engineer who spent 6.5 years climbing from Materials & Process Engineer → IMC Materials Coordinator → IMC Materials Leader at SLB. ### What SRPC Is (Why It Matters)
SRPC (Schlumberger Riboud Product Centre) in Clamart is **SLB's largest technology hub in Europe and second largest worldwide** — ~1,300 employees, 60+ nationalities, founded 1959. It is the nerve centre of SLB's European R&D. Mohamad is not at a small satellite office; he is at the most important engineering site SLB has outside North America. **If AM gets adopted at SRPC, it rolls out globally across SLB's entire operation.**

### What SLB Actually Does With AM (Public Intel)
- **Aegis Armor (flagship AM product):** SLB uses EBM (electron beam powder bed fusion) through their Smith Bits division to print a tungsten carbide armor cladding onto PDC drill bit blades — +40% blade strength, +400% erosion resistance, up to 36% faster ROP. Commercially deployed in 5 shale basins + Europe, Middle East, South America.
- **Roboze PEEK partnership (Jan 2025):** SLB signed an MoU with Roboze to create **on-demand local AM production of PEEK and Carbon Fiber PEEK parts** in Saudi Arabia — reducing logistics dependency for downhole components. This started in 2021, MoU signed at IKTVA Forum 2025. **This is directly relevant to you — SLB has already validated the "on-demand local production" concept.**
- **Mohamad's LPBF work:** SLB has not made public announcements about LPBF specifically. Mohamad's LPBF focus at SRPC is likely internal qualification R&D — new alloys, new geometries, new applications for European and global deployment. The 11,000+ parts milestone he reposted (Safran, not SLB) signals he tracks industry-wide LPBF progress closely.
- **IMC = Integrated Manufacturing Centre:** SLB's IMC function bridges R&D and production — it is the team that takes a promising AM result from the lab and makes it manufacturable at scale. Mohamad leading Materials for this function means he controls the materials qualification pipeline that gates what SLB can and cannot print.

**Q1 — Qualification bottleneck**
You've scaled to 11,000+ LPBF parts at SLB's SRPC — that's serious production, not a pilot. Where does the qualification chain most often break down or slow you down: internal approvals, class society timelines, or missing materials data?

**Q2 — Digital file & IP**
When a field operation needs a part that can only be printed at an external facility, how does SLB protect the digital file and certify the output? Is there a formal DRM process, or is it contracts and trust — and where has that broken down?

**Q3 — Materials data gap**
When qualifying a new alloy for LPBF at SLB, is the mechanical property data (yield, fatigue, elongation) already there, or are you building it from scratch every time? Would a shared, class-society-validated AM materials database actually save you time, or does SLB prefer to own that data internally?

**Q4 — External AM network**
When a production-stopped emergency happens somewhere SLB's Paris facility can't reach in time — what actually happens today? Is there a vetted network of external certified printers you can route work to, or is it still "ship from Paris and wait"?

**Q5 — The real adoption barrier**
You've been inside SLB's AM operation for 6+ years. What's the honest reason AM adoption in O&G is still slower than the technology deserves — and what would it actually take to break through?
