### Q1: The Supplier Qualification Gauntlet — What Does It Actually Cost WESCO to Onboard a New Specialized Supplier?

> **"WESCO operates at a scale where supplier qualification is a serious organizational process, not a phone call. I know from the outside that large distributors like WESCO maintain approved vendor lists, quality standards, insurance requirements, and performance criteria that any new supplier has to meet before getting their first order. Walk me through what that process actually looks like for a specialized or custom manufacturer — say, a precision fabrication shop or a specialized 3D printing facility — that WESCO has never worked with before. How long does it take, what does it require, and what does it cost your organization to run that process?"**

**Probes:**
- In your current role or in your Director/Senior Director experience, how many times have you or your team onboarded a genuinely new, previously unknown specialized supplier in a 12-month period? Is it rare, occasional, or routine?
- Your pharmaceutical background means you've seen supplier qualification at its most rigorous — GMP compliance, validation protocols, batch records. How does WESCO's qualification process compare in structure and rigor? Is there a documented standard, or does it vary by category and buyer?
- When WESCO's industrial clients (utilities, construction companies, manufacturers) ask WESCO to source a component it doesn't currently have a qualified supplier for — what happens? Does WESCO absorb the qualification work, or does it push back to the client and say "not available through our current vendor network"?
- What are the three or four most common failure modes when a new specialized supplier goes through WESCO's qualification process? Is it documentation, insurance, financial viability, quality certifications, capacity uncertainty, or something else? At what stage do most candidates drop out?
- If you could reduce the time and cost of qualifying a new specialized supplier by 70% — using pre-verified supplier profiles with standardized certification data — would that actually change how often your team considers adding new suppliers, or are the constraints elsewhere (risk appetite, category manager conservatism, preferred vendor program contracts)?

**Why this matters:** Tests CS.1 directly — whether the friction and time cost of sourcing from new local suppliers is a real and measurable operational problem at the enterprise buyer level, or whether existing procurement channels already handle this efficiently.

Has that pressure reached your operations team yet in a tangible way?

Drawing on that experience: when a client needed a custom component with no standard catalogue solution, how did the sourcing process work? Was there ever a situation where capability existed locally but wasn't findable?

How much capacity do you think sits on the table that way?

Would it accelerate qualification, or does the process still take the same amount of time regardless of upfront verification?

**Why this matters:** Tests CS.2 — whether capable specialized local manufacturing suppliers are genuinely stranded and inaccessible to enterprise buyers, not because they lack capability, but because the discovery and qualification infrastructure doesn't connect them.

### Q3: Procurement Manager Incentives — Why Caution Is Rational and What Changes That Calculus

> **"I want to talk about the human side of procurement decisions. In my conversations with supply chain executives, a pattern keeps emerging: the procurement manager who tries a new, unknown supplier and it goes wrong — late delivery, quality failure, a customer upset — faces real career consequences. The one who sticks with the same vendor for the tenth year and gets mediocre service barely gets noticed. That asymmetry means the rational behavior for an individual procurement manager is almost always to stick with the known rather than take a chance on a local supplier they've never used. In your direct operational experience — is that pattern real at WESCO, and broadly in industrial distribution?"**

**Probes:**
- In your 17 years at WESCO across roles from distribution supervisor to RVP, have you witnessed or experienced that exact penalty asymmetry — where trying a new supplier and facing a problem had visible career consequences, but staying with an underperforming known vendor didn't? Is it cultural, systemic, or does it depend on the leader?
- From your pharmaceutical operations background, you know specification-driven procurement where a supplier failure can have serious downstream consequences (patient safety). Did that environment create a culture of hyper-caution around new suppliers that followed you into industrial distribution? Or did you unlearn some of that?
- At WESCO's scale, are there formal metrics that track supplier diversification, new supplier additions, or "local content" of sourcing? Or are the primary KPIs entirely on cost, service level, and on-time delivery — which systematically disadvantage new supplier adoption?
- What organizational design or incentive structure would actually change procurement manager behaviour toward new supplier adoption — particularly toward smaller, local specialized suppliers — without creating unacceptable service risk? Are there examples from WESCO's own evolution where a leadership decision shifted that culture?
- When you look at WESCO's top-performing regional operations, do you observe a pattern where more agile supplier adoption correlates with better outcomes — either lower costs, faster response to customer needs, or more resilient supply chains? Or does the correlation go the other way — stability and deep vendor relationships outperform agility every time?

**Why this matters:** Surfaces the deep behavioural and incentive barriers that sit underneath CS.1 — even if procurement managers know a better local supplier exists, organizational incentives may make it rational to ignore it. Lab2Market's design needs to reduce not just search cost but also the perceived risk of actioning the search result.

### Q4: The 2026 Tariff World — Is "Buy Canadian" Pressure Creating a Real Window for Local Specialized Suppliers?

> **"We're having this conversation in March 2026. Canada's trade relationship with the US has fundamentally changed — US Section 232 tariffs, Canadian counter-tariffs, 'Buy Canadian' pressure from federal and provincial governments, and the beginning of genuine industrial reshoring conversations. WESCO Canada operates in this environment right now. I want to understand from your operational vantage point: is that macro pressure actually translating into real changes in how WESCO Canada sources from its supplier base? Or are procurement teams still defaulting to the same vendors because the operational risk of change outweighs the political noise?"**

**Probes:**
- Has WESCO Canada received formal direction from corporate leadership or from major industrial clients to increase Canadian content in its sourcing — and if so, is that direction coming with real procurement budget tied to it, or is it a mandate without a mechanism?
- In specific product categories that WESCO distributes — electrical components, industrial hardware, connectors, custom-fabricated housing — which categories have the most realistic near-term pathway to Canadian substitution, and which are structurally locked to offshore sourcing regardless of tariff pressure?
- Your background in supply chain operations means you think about lead time risk, cost, and service levels, not just geopolitics. In your honest operational assessment: for WESCO Canada, is the tariff environment making a serious business case for qualifying more Canadian specialized suppliers, or is the math still firmly in favor of established offshore supply chains despite the tariff cost?
- You've managed through supply chain disruptions — COVID proved that just-in-time is brittle. Has that experience shifted internal thinking at WESCO Canada about the value of maintaining qualified local or Canadian alternatives, even at a modest cost premium, as supply chain insurance? Or has the organization largely reverted to pre-COVID cost-optimization as the primary driver?
- If you were advising a small Canadian advanced manufacturing facility — precision fabrication, 3D printing, specialized metalwork — on how to position itself to win its first contract with a WESCO-tier industrial buyer in 2026: what are the three things it must get right, and what are the two biggest mistakes it will likely make?

**Why this matters:** Tests whether the macro environment (tariffs, reshoring, Buy Canadian) is creating a real and near-term market pull for Lab2Market's platform — or whether structural procurement inertia means the policy tailwind doesn't translate into changed buyer behavior on the ground in 2026.
