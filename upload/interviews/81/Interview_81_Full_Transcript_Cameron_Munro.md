If we wanted a small production run—even just half a dozen parts—it would cost hundreds of thousands of dollars just to have a company create the tooling necessary to make those parts through traditional casting or forging.

**Mahmoud:** That makes sense. I've had meetings with many industries and procurement and certification bodies have been major issues. **Cameron:** These are very difficult questions—big picture. We don't have really clear, concise guidance from Lloyd's and ABS on the implementation or acceptance of AM parts. We ask, "Well, what do you need to manage the risk of putting a newly manufactured part—made using a new technique—into service?"

Usually, that involves showing that you meet the design intent of the original part. It often involves doing the same kind of testing you would do on a traditional casting or forging, but maybe just doing a few more test samples to make sure there's consistency from build to build in AM.

On the IP question—that can be an issue if we're talking about systems that still have a manufacturer in business. But in a lot of cases, we're dealing with systems that are 30-plus years old. The original manufacturer may no longer be in business. In that case, there's a lot of reverse engineering that needs to be done. When that's done, Canada owns the drawings. We're not going to export the IP, but we're going to use the drawings and reverse engineering results to manufacture replacement parts. In some cases, you just can't buy parts anymore for these systems.

Yes, there are serious questions around who owns the IP and whether we can make a supplier provide drawings to us. In a lot of cases, we can't—our agreements with them don't say they must turn over drawings. So that's where we work with reverse engineering to try and answer those questions. But yes, it can be problematic if the company is still in business.
