## Strategic Context: Why This Project Matters

### Victoria-Class Submarine Fleet Challenge
- **Age Profile:** Commissioned 1988–1998; average age 20+ years (into mid-life refresh phase)
- **Operational Plan:** Extended service life into 2050s+ requires long-term spare parts availability
- **Original Supply Chain:** Many suppliers from 1980s have exited market, been acquired, or lost technical expertise
- **Component Availability:** Risk of critical spares becoming unavailable exactly when fleet is operationally important
- **Cost Implication:** Expedited procurement and premium pricing when spares are scarce

### Why Additive Manufacturing?
1. **Supplier Consolidation:** AM allows Navy to source from new, competitively-vetted suppliers instead of locked-in monopolies
2. **On-Demand Production:** Reduces inventory carry costs and lead times for critical components
3. **Domestic Capacity:** Builds Canadian capability; reduces reliance on foreign suppliers
4. **Design Ownership:** Navy can evolve component designs without supplier approval
5. **Cost Efficiency:** In many cases, 3D printing reduces material waste and manufacturing complexity

### Strategic Defence Implications
- **Operational Continuity:** Ensures submarine fleet availability when needed
- **Sovereign Capability:** Reduces dependence on foreign governments or commercial suppliers
- **Allied Interoperability:** Model could extend to NATO allies and Five Eyes nations
- **Industrial Capacity:** Builds Canadian advanced manufacturing expertise and infrastructure
- **Precedent & Leadership:** Demonstrates defence R&D can drive commercial innovation ecosystems
