## CRITICAL PAIN POINTS IDENTIFIED

### 1. **SAG Mills as Single Point of Failure**
- **Context:** SAG (Semi-Autogenous Grinding) mills are the bottleneck in 40-60% of mining operations
- **Size:** 40-60 feet diameter equipment
- **Components:** Massive motors, agitators, structural components
- **Problem:** Bespoke, custom-built—no off-the-shelf suppliers
- **Supply Chain**: Single-source suppliers, long lead times (months to years)
- **Impact:** One SAG mill failure = operational shutdown = millions in lost revenue

### 2. **Remote Site Supply Chain Complexity**
- **Russia Case Study:** 2-3 year supply chains due to geography
  - Ice roads available only 4 months/year
  - Parts shipped from US, Canada, China, Taiwan
  - Staged at port towns, wait 3-6 months for ice roads
  - Final delivery to remote site
  - Must last 12-16 months with minimal slack
- **Current Global Sites:** South America, Chile, Brazil, Africa, Alaska
- **Lesson:** Extreme remoteness = extreme lead times = extreme costs

### 3. **Spare Parts Inventory Paradox**
- **Example:** $100M+ critical spare held on-site
- **Reason:** Must remain available for own operations
- **Competitor Request Denied:** Kinross had spare, competitor needed it, but couldn't loan (risk of own downtime)
- **Financial Reality:** Better to hold expensive spares than risk production shutdown
- **Implication:** 15-20% margins on parts acceptable for reliability

### 4. **Operational Resilience = Board-Level Priority**
- **Drivers:**
  - Gold commodity price volatility ($2,000 → $5,000 → $4,500 in 3 years)
  - Need to maximize production when prices are favorable
  - Downtime during peak-price windows = catastrophic ROI loss
- **Current Track Record:** No major disruptions in past 5-6 years (through proactive management)
- **Risk Management:** Holding expensive spares, maintaining expertise on-site, building supply networks in advance
- **CEO Focus:** Resiliency planning is "high focus today"

## ADDITIVE MANUFACTURING ASSESSMENT

### **Past Experience: Russian Operations**
- **Challenge:** 2-3 year supply chains made traditional procurement impossible
- **Solution:** 3D printing, CNC machining, specialized lathing
- **Success Factors:**
  - Hot-shot parts via 4-hour Arctic flights (expensive but necessary)
  - In-house expertise to machine and fabricate alternatives
  - Creative problem-solving culture
- **Outcome:** Successfully maintained uptime in one of world's most remote, constrained supply chains

### **Current Receptiveness to 3D Printing**
✅ **Positive Indicators:**
- Already experimented with AM (not hypothetical)
- CEO-level openness to innovation
- Clear pain points that AM could solve
- Track record of rapid adjustment to extreme supply chain challenges

❌ **Major Barriers:**

#### **Barrier 1: Technical Capability Concern**
- **Question:** Can 3D-printed parts meet high-stress, heat-treated requirements?
- **Context:** Mining equipment undergoes:
  - Quenching and heat-treating
  - Secondary surface treatments
  - Extreme stress environments (grinding forces, thermal cycles)
- **Kobalch's Position:** "If you can guarantee technical capability, I'm in"
- **Implication:** Material science + metallurgical validation REQUIRED before mining companies adopt

#### **Barrier 2: Liability & Warranty**
- **Challenge:** If 3D-printed part fails on warranted equipment, who carries liability?
- **Stakeholders Affected:**
  - Equipment OEM (warranty obligation)
  - Kinross (operational impact)
  - 3D printing vendor (manufacturing trust)
  - InsuranceProviders (coverage ambiguity)
- **Kobalch's Position:** Critical blocker until resolved
- **Implication:** Legal/contractual framework must be established BEFORE deployment

#### **Barrier 3: Workforce Complexity**
- **Question:** Simple "print button" or requires specialized engineers?
- **Context:** 
  - Kinross operates across remote sites (South America, Chile, Brazil, Africa, Alaska)
  - Each site has local workforce with varying technical capabilities
  - Training burden could offset cost savings
- **Kobalch's Position:** Ease-of-use is critical for adoption in remote mining operations
- **Two Scenarios:**
  - ✅ Simple operation (ChatGPT-like guidance) = scalable
  - ❌ Requires specialized engineers = limits deployment

## COMPETITIVE POSITIONING INSIGHTS

### **Kobalch on OEM Revenue Models**
- **Current Model:** OEMs make profit on spare parts (not equipment)
  - PlayStation: Make $0 on console, profits on games
  - Caterpillar: Make $0-10% on truck, 15-20% on parts and service
  - Incumbent suppliers: Built business on parts monopoly
  
- **Disruption Risk:** Opening up CAD designs and distributed manufacturing threatens OEM revenue
  - **Kobalch Question:** How does OEM transition from "parts monopoly" to "manufacturing partner"?
  - **Insight:** Need OEM incentive structure that protects revenue while enabling distributed manufacturing

### **Kinross Perspective:** Cost + Trust > Brand Loyalty
- Would Kinross pay 15-20% margins if it meant reliability? YES
- Would Kinross abandon incumbent suppliers for cheaper, riskier unknown? NO
- **Sweet Spot:** Same reliability + some cost savings + reduced lead times = compelling
