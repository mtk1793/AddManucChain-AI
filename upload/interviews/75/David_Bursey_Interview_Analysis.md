## KEY INTERVIEW INSIGHTS

### THEME 1: Offshore Pressure Systems Fail in Predictable, High-Cost Ways

**David's Three Primary Failure Modes:**

1. **Corrosion-Related Installation Degradation**
   - *Most Complex Scenario:* Pipe coatings fail, corrosion penetrates insulation, material loss occurs
   - *The Real Problem:* This creates DUAL risk — both integrity of pressure boundary AND structural support for the pipe
   - *Consequence:* Can't rebuild insulation in-situ; requires temporary repair to plan permanent fix
   - *Cost Impact:* Temporary repair + later permanent repair = extended downtime, multiple interventions

2. **Pressure Vessel Repairs in Confined Spaces**
   - *Challenge:* Offshore facilities are severely space-constrained (entire hydrocarbon plant "crammed in the deck of a boat")
   - *Standard Solution:* Cut out damaged section, weld in new piece
   - *The Real Cost:* Welding repairs are limited in scope and require extreme precision in cramped spaces
   - *Timeline:* Requires full plant shutdown to replace component safely

3. **Mechanical Clamps & Alternative Repairs Under Constraint**
   - *When Welding Isn't Possible:* Use specialized mechanical clamps (custom-designed for specific failure mode)
   - *Fiber-Glass Wrap Solutions:* Covered under ASME/API standards for temporary repairs
   - *Why This Matters:* Shows desperation for non-invasive repair solutions

**Key Quote:** *"We operate an offshore gas facility in an offshore environment where repair techniques are limited. Welding repairs are limited in scope, so we have to come up with other means."*

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- Pressure system repair is a **high-value, high-frequency problem** in Canadian offshore operations
- **Custom specialized parts** (clamps, adapters, replacements) are more valuable than commodity parts initially
- But the **quick-win entry point is small commodity parts** (bolts, valve seats) that are causing emergency situations due to supply delays

---

### THEME 2: Supply Chain Delays Measure in Weeks; Emergency Access is Critical

**The Real Supply Chain Problem:**

David described a **cascading supply chain failure** where even simple components have become impossible to access on-demand:

**The Duplex Stainless Problem:**
*"We use 25% Chrome duplex stainless steel or 22% Chrome duplex stainless steel for production piping. In the last 6 years since COVID, our supply chains have gotten really slow. What was once available in weeks now takes months."*

**The Valve Component Crisis:**
*"We have discovered that some of these valves going to have long lead times which can be an endurance to remain his programme."*

**The Immediate Crisis Example (This Morning!):**
*"I had an example even this morning... the specified bolts that I needed for pressure vessel inspection and repair weren't available offshore. It took a week and a half to get them from the supplier. Just simple nuts and bolts."*

**The Material Substitution Trap:**
When proper materials aren't available, David is forced into risky workarounds:
- Substitute with "lesser grade" materials (e.g., Stainless 316 instead of 22 Chrome Duplex)
- Compensate with increased inspection frequency
- Accept higher risk until proper materials arrive
- Document everything for regulatory compliance

**Key Quote:** *"We will not use lesser grade material unless we confirm that it's very temporary. We have to be very scrutinized and closely managed."*

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- **Supply chain delays create decision windows where on-site replacement becomes viable**
- **7-10 day delay on simple components** creates enough margin for on-site printing (if print time is 1-3 days)
- **The economic justification is strong:** Printing small bolt/component locally may cost more per unit but saves $100K+ in downtime costs per day
- **This is an O&G-specific, regulated problem** where the risk is literally lives and environment, making on-time delivery premium-worthy

### THEME 3: Risk-Based vs. Predictive Maintenance: A Qualitative Consensus Model

**David's Maintenance Philosophy:**

Current system: **API 580 Risk-Based Qualitative Inspection Programme** (NOT deep numerical analytics)

*"We don't go on the deep numerical analytics. We get all the particular stakeholders of an asset together. We have a discussion and come to a consensus on what we think the risk is, what the consequence of failure could be, what are reasonable time frames for inspection."*

**Key Insight:** This is **deliberately conservative, collaborative, and consensus-driven**—not optimized for minimum inspection.

**Implication for AddManuChain AI/Predictive Features:**
- David's current approach is **qualitative stakeholder consensus**, not data-driven prediction
- **There's a gap** between current practice (conservative consensus) and potential AI-driven prediction
- **Opportunity:** Build AI predictive model that IMPROVES on current qualitative approach while respecting regulatory conservatism
- **Warning:** David won't adopt numeric prediction unless it reduces risk AND reduces inspection burden, not just optimizes cost

### THEME 4: Regulatory Certification is Achievable but Time-Consuming

**The Regulatory Path:**

David described the certification hierarchy:
1. **Primary Regulator:** Canada-Newfoundland and Labrador Energy Regulator (CNLER)
2. **Verification Societies (acting on behalf of CNLER):** DNV, Lloyd's, American Bureau of Shipping
3. **Vendor Process:** Approach verification society with new product/process → Testing + Certification → Can approach potential clients

**David's Assessment:**
*"It kind of reduces our risk, especially when it comes using new products, new technologies. So typically, if the vendor wants to get certificate, get their equipment or process certified, they can approach the DNV, and say, 'We bought this new widget, how do we test it? And how do we get certification?'"*

**The Reality:** Certification is possible but takes time and cost (vendor must pay for verification society testing/witnessing)

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- **Certification pathway for AM is NOT a blocker**—it's a time/cost investment
- **Priority:** Build partnerships with DNV and Lloyd's to pre-certify AM processes for O&G applications
- **Regulatory argument:** If process is DNV-certified, CNLER/clients will accept it faster
- **This should be a product feature:** "Certified AM processes for regulated industries" = premium positioning

### THEME 5: Proprietary Parts Are the Real Digital Inventory Barrier

**The Strategic Problem David Identified:**

When asked about digital inventory concept, David pivoted to a deeper issue:

*"I think a lot of these parts would be proprietary to the vendors who sold the doors. It would include a lot of patented proprietary components from all of our different vendors, which if we could get around that would be a very big win for us because we pay a lot of money for spare parts from these vendors. Yeah, they may not be so reluctant to give it up."*

**Why This Matters:**
- Cenovus has **heterogeneous supply chain** (different vendors for different subsystems)
- Each vendor guards design and specs as competitive advantage/lock-in mechanism
- Cenovus can't build a unified digital parts library without vendor cooperation
- **Cost Impact:** Vendor monopoly on replacement parts = significant capex/opex burden

**The Workaround David Identified:**
*"Certain valves and bolts... they may be generic enough. You could build a library that way. Certains and balls. You know, it sounds like a simple item."*

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- **Start with commodity/generic components** (bolts, fasteners, standard valve seats) — they're not locked up by IP
- **This is your entry point**, not custom complex parts
- **But the long-term vision is compelling:** Help customers reverse-engineer/document proprietary parts (with vendor permission) to build complete digital library
- **This becomes a key negotiation point with vendors:** "Want to lock customers in forever? Or help us modernize supply chains and increase utilization?"

### THEME 6: Small Parts + Automatic Inventory Management = Compelling Use Case

**David's Vision (Emerged Near End of Interview):**

*"If we carry a small stock offshore, and we keep what's called a 'safety stock.' So that when the amount of people of material inventory drops to a certain point, there's an automatic reward to refill it. But instead of refilling in the logistics of shipping stuff onshore, if we could print components offshore in a way of just being an automatic process, and when inventory drops, the printer kicks in and produces more... that would be fantastic."*

**This is the AddManuChain Use Case Exactly:**
1. Small parts library (bolts, seals, adapters, valve components)
2. Inventory thresholds trigger automatic restocking
3. Instead of ordering from vendor (7-10 day delay), local printing (1-3 day cycle)
4. Dramatically reduces downtime on critical infrastructure

**The Economics:**
- Cost of one day offshore downtime: **~$100,000+**
- Cost of on-site printing: **~$500-2,000 per part**
- ROI on printing capability: **Saves money on first emergency**

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- This is your **killer use case for O&G**: Automatic inventory management with on-site printing
- **The narrative:** "Don't wait for vendors. Print when you need."
- **The regulatory angle:** "Printed parts must be certified for pressure systems" (solvable via DNV certification)

### THEME 8: Emergency Sourcing is a Known, Accepted, High-Cost Reality

**David's Emergency Response Pattern:**

When critical components aren't available:
1. **Assess fitness-for-service** with substitutes
2. **Implement temporary repair** (mechanical clamp, fiber-glass wrap)
3. **Increase monitoring/inspection** frequency
4. **Plan permanent repair** during next maintenance window
5. **Document extensively** for regulatory compliance

**Example:** Valve seats/components with long lead times force this entire cycle.

**The Insight:** David considers this **normal operations**, not exceptional. The system is designed to handle supply failures, but it's expensive and risky.

**Implication for AddManuChain:**
- Companies like Cenovus **already budget for emergency sourcing costs**
- On-site printing could convert some of these emergency costs into planned capex
- **Regulatory bodies understand supply constraints** — they're pragmatic about temporary measures
- **Certified AM sourcing becomes an alternative to temporary repairs** (better risk profile)

## RED FLAGS & RISKS IDENTIFIED

1. **Vendor Lock-in is a Business Model Threat**
   - Vendors may not cooperate with digital parts libraries
   - *Mitigation:* Partner with customers directly; show vendors that digital inventory increases overall market utilization

2. **Proprietary Part Complexity**
   - 80% of parts may be vendor-proprietary, not amenable to digital library
   - *Mitigation:* Start with commodity parts (20% of SKU base) where volumes are highest; expand gradually

3. **Regulatory Certification Timeline**
   - DNV/Lloyd's certification can take 6-12 months per material/process combination
   - *Mitigation:* Build partnerships with verification societies; start certification process early; pre-certify common materials

4. **Printing Speed vs. Emergency Timeline**
   - If print time exceeds 2-3 days, value prop weakens vs. emergency air freight
   - *Mitigation:* Focus on commodity parts with lead-time demand (high-turnover SKUs); not breakthrough emergen­cies

5. **Logistics Integration**
   - Getting equipment to offshore facilities is complex; regulatory approval for new equipment on rigs
   - *Mitigation:* Partner with offshore service providers; explore modular printing systems; start with onshore applications
