## Key Interview Questions & Responses

### Question 1: Critical Supply Chain Bottlenecks

**Q:** What is the single most expensive supply chain problem for critical spare parts across the portfolio?

**A - SAG Mills as Critical Constraint:**
- **Most critical item:** Semi-autogenous (SAG) mills
- **Why:** Both long lead times AND single-source supplier risk
- **Specifications:** 40-60 feet diameter with massive components
  - Massive motors, agitators, structural components
  - Bespoke, made-to-measure equipment
- **Operational reality:** Based on "Theory of Constraints" - milling/grinding is the bottleneck
- **Secondary critical items:** Any component related to crushing and grinding rock

**Insight:** The bottleneck is not in raw material supply but in specialized, large-scale equipment with complex specifications that cannot be easily substituted.

---

### Question 2: Operational Disruptions & Financial Impact

**Q:** In past 24-36 months, how many critical operations have been disrupted due to spare part supply chain issues? What was the operational/financial impact?

**A - Managed Disruptions:**
- **Success rate:** 5-6 years with NO major operational disruptions
- **Disruption management expertise:** Through strategic spare parts inventory and supplier networks
  - Critical spares maintained on-site
  - Pre-established supply chain networks with main dealers
  - In-house technical expertise to reduce downtime from months to weeks

**Specific example:** Competitor was down 3-4 months due to critical spare unavailability; would have taken 6 months to machine replacement

**Resilience investment:** $10 million critical spare on hand (proactive risk management)

**Current risk focus:**
- **PEARL approach to risk analysis:** People, Environmental, Assets, Reputation, Livelihood impacts
- **Top concern:** Commodity price volatility (gold at $2,000-$5,000 fluctuations)
- **Resilience planning:** High priority given current commodity environment

**Insight:** This company has invested heavily in proactive supply chain management, but still recognizes resilience as critical given operational scale and commodity volatility.

### Question 3: Additive Manufacturing Perspective

**Q:** Have you considered 3D printing as a solution for spare parts?

**A - Active Exploration with Real Experience:**
- **Experience level:** Already experimented with 3D printing and CNC machining
- **Context:** Russia operations had extreme supply chain constraints
  - 2-3 year supply chain due to Arctic operations
  - Ice roads only passable 4 months/year
  - Multi-port shipping requiring 3-6 month waits
  - 12-16 month supply provisions needed
- **Solutions attempted:**
  - 3D printing for equipment uptime
  - Specialized CNC machinery and lathing
  - Hot-shot parts via 4-hour Arctic flights (extremely expensive)
- **Status:** "Very creative" solutions developed but unclear if acceleration occurred in last 5-6 years

**Insight:** This executive has FIRST-HAND experience with 3D printing as a supply chain solution and understands its potential, but also understands the limitations and constraints.

### Question 4: $10M Supply Chain Resilience Investment

**Q:** If you had $10M annual envelope for supply chain resilience, what's your first investment to generate measurable ROI in 12-18 months?

**A - People and Training Priority:**
- **Investment preference:** **PEOPLE AND TRAINING** (not technology)
- **Reasoning:**
  - Spend on people across ALL areas: supply chain, planning, operations
  - Better people → better process execution
  - Better people → better strategy implementation
- **Context:** Department spends ~$800M/year, so $10M is significant
- **Implicit message:** Technology investments are limited by human capability to execute them

**Insight:** Despite being involved in AI/innovation initiatives, this executive prioritizes human capital and organizational capability as the bottleneck for supply chain resilience. This suggests technology alone won't solve problems; integration and execution are critical.

### Question 5: Platform Concept Feedback

**Proposed Concept:** Digital inventory platform with on-site 3D printing capability

**Key Value Propositions Presented:**
1. On-site 3D printers for parts with weeks/months lead times
2. Significant cost savings (vs. major downtime costs)
3. Digital inventory managed through OEM partnerships & certification bodies
4. Scalable: move parts to nearest 3D printing facility if needed
5. AI integration for predictive maintenance + inventory management

**Feedback - Balanced Enthusiasm with Critical Concerns:**

✅ **What Works:**
- "It sounds very interesting. I'm really happy you're studying this."
- Strong interest in on-demand printing capability
- Recognition that open-source approach could disrupt vendor margins (15-20%)
- Genuine curiosity about the business model

⚠️ **Critical Barriers Identified:**

**1. Technical Capability Concerns:**
- **Can 3D printed parts meet high-strength requirements?**
  - Industry uses quenched, heat-treated components
  - Secondary treatment facilities apply specialized surface treatments
  - Question: Can 3D printed parts achieve same technical capabilities?
  - This is a DEALBREAKER if not addressed

**2. Liability & Warranty Issues:**
- Who carries liability if 3D printed part fails?
- Complex when parts go onto warranted equipment
- Liability chain unclear: manufacturer, technology provider, OEM?
- This must be legally resolved before implementation

**3. Operational Complexity - Ease of Use:**
- Operations span: South America, Chile, Brazil, Africa, Alaska
- Workforce question: Do I need specialized mechanical engineers to operate printer?
- Or is it truly "push button" simple?
- Concern about workforce expansion requirements
- This affects total cost of ownership significantly

**4. Business Model Risk to Existing Vendors:**
- Industry majors (Caterpillar, Komatsu) make money on parts (not equipment)
- PlayStation model: lose money on hardware, profit on software/games
- How would vendors respond to open-source threat to their service revenue?

**Executive's Timeline:** "These are 3 things I want answered fairly quickly before proceeding"

**Insight:** This is a warm lead with serious interest, BUT there are THREE specific gatekeeping questions that must be answered before moving from innovation discussion to actual project consideration.

## Key Insights & Validation

### ✅ Problem Validation

1. **Supply chain resilience IS a top priority** - especially for commodity businesses
2. **Spare parts availability is genuinely critical** - not just inconvenient
3. **Long lead times on specialized equipment IS a major cost driver**
4. **Current solutions are expensive and complex** (spare inventories worth millions)
5. **3D printing is already being explored** - but gaps remain unaddressed

### ✅ Market Size Indicator

- Single company operates 6 sites, 3 projects, 20,000 employees
- $800M annual supply chain spend
- $10M spare equipment on-site (just for backup)
- Global mining operations spread across multiple continents
- Large enough portfolio that millions in downtime is plausible

### ⚠️ Critical Implementation Gaps

1. **Technical qualification:** Must prove 3D printed parts meet industry standards (heat treatment, metallurgical properties)
2. **Certification path:** Need clearance from regulatory bodies and OEM partners
3. **Liability framework:** Legal structure for shared responsibility must be established
4. **Workforce training:** Usability/training burden must be quantified
5. **Vendor relationships:** OEM partnerships must be formalized - they may resist

### 💡 Strategic Insights

1. **People > Technology:** Even innovation leaders prioritize human capability over tech - need strong implementation teams
2. **Trust and advisors matter:** Executive emphasized quality of advisory relationships - this is a relationship-driven market
3. **Integration, not disruption:** Approach should complement existing systems, not replace them entirely
4. **Industry standards are real:** High-safety industries have strict qualification requirements - not optional
5. **Commodity-driven urgency:** When gold prices drop, resilience becomes more important (less margin for error)
