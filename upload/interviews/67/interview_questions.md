# Interview Questions — Niraj Shukla (Interview #67)

**Interviewee:** Niraj Shukla  
**Position:** IP, Innovation & Partnerships Builder, Springboard Atlantic Inc.  
**Date:** TBD | **Interviewer:** Mahmoud Kiasari

## SNAPSHOT

**Profile:** One of Atlantic Canada's central IP commercialization practitioners. Niraj operates at the boundary between university research, start-up formation, government policy, and industry procurement. Through Springboard Atlantic's 19-institution network and his ElevateIP/CIPO connections, he sees first-hand where Atlantic Canadian innovation stalls before reaching industrial buyers — and what breaks those logjams. He has publicly engaged with Canada's new Defence Industrial Strategy, reading it as a mandate for IP-anchored, domestic-first procurement.

**Interview Goal:** Understand the IP and trust dimensions blocking Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing suppliers from connecting with industrial buyers — and whether Springboard's network is a viable channel for Lab2Market's go-to-market. Core research areas: IP as a buyer confidence signal; the research-lab-to-buyer gap; how federal programs like ElevateIP relate to supplier certification; the "Build-Partner-Buy" policy shift; and what a small 3D printing facility actually needs to get its first commercial order.

**Research Areas:**
- IP verification and trust — does owning and protecting IP make a supplier more credible to buyers?
- Research-to-industry supply gap — how well-connected are Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing labs to industrial procurement teams?
- Federal programs and supplier readiness — what ElevateIP, NRC, and Innovation Asset Collective actually do or don't do for supplier commercialization
- Defence Industrial Strategy — how real is the "Buy Canadian" / "Build-Partner-Buy" mandate in terms of practical procurement change?
- Springboard Atlantic as a platform channel — viability of reaching both supply and demand side through post-secondary networks

### Q1: The IP Layer in Supplier Selection — Does It Actually Move Buyers?

> **"You've spent years helping Canadian SMEs and research spinouts build IP positions that are supposed to translate into commercial outcomes. in your experience advising start-ups and working with industry partners, does a clear IP position — a filed patent, a documented trade secret, a verified process certification — actually change how an industrial buyer evaluates a new supplier? Or is it still fundamentally a relationships game where IP is nice-to-have but not a deal-mover?"**

**Probes:**
- When you've worked with Atlantic Canadian start-ups trying to land their first industrial customer, what role did IP ownership or IP documentation play in that conversation? Was it: (a) a requirement to even start discussions, (b) a credibility signal that helped but wasn't necessary, (c) something buyers brought up proactively, or (d) largely irrelevant to the deal?
- You reposted the Springboard summary of the software IP webinar where the key message was: "File early. Don't disclose before protecting. Limit disclosure to non-enabling information." That guidance is aimed at founders. But does the corresponding behaviour — a supplier who has clearly protected and documented their process — change how a procurement manager approaches the relationship?
- In industries like aerospace, defence, and energy — where you have ITAR, export controls, and national security overlays — does IP documentation shift from "nice to have" to a hard procurement requirement? Have you seen cases in Atlantic Canada where IP status was the deciding factor in whether a supplier got onto an approved vendor list?
- You've been engaged with the Innovation Asset Collective's IP Strategy Program, which funds applications from Canadian SMEs building IP positions tied to commercial outcomes. In practice, of the SMEs you've seen go through programs like this, how many come out the other side with a supplier relationship or a commercial contract? What's the conversion rate from "IP developed" to "first order received"?
- If Lab2Market were to create a "verified IP position" badge as part of its supplier credentialing — confirming that a facility has clear IP ownership over its processes — would that carry real weight with industrial buyers in your network, or would it be perceived as a startup-ecosystem credential that doesn't translate to enterprise procurement?

**Why this matters:** Tests VP.5 — whether third-party verification and credentialing actually shifts buyer behavior toward suppliers they haven't worked with, with a specific lens on IP as a trust signal in Atlantic Canada's industrial procurement ecosystem.

### Q2: The Research-to-Industry Gap — How Stranded Is Atlantic Canadian Advanced Manufacturing Capacity?

> **"Springboard Atlantic connects 19 post-secondary institutions to industry. On paper, that should mean industrial buyers can access cutting-edge research-lab manufacturing capacity in Atlantic Canada — including additive manufacturing, advanced materials, specialized fabrication. But in practice — honestly, not theoretically — how often does that actually happen? How many of those 19 institutions have research manufacturing capacity that's commercially accessible to an industrial buyer who needs a custom part or a small production run?"**

**Probes:**
- When you think about Memorial University, Dalhousie, UNB, NSCC, and the other institutions in Springboard's network — how many of them have additive manufacturing or advanced fabrication capacity that is theoretically available to an external industrial buyer? And of those, how many have ever actually fulfilled a commercial order from outside academia?
- What's the typical pathway when a company approaches a post-secondary institution asking if their lab can fulfill a manufacturing need? Is there a standard process, or is it entirely dependent on knowing the right person?
- You've noted that the "Build-Partner-Buy" framework and ElevateIP are designed to close commercial gaps. But is the gap on the supply side (university labs and SMEs that have the capability but are trapped by IP uncertainty, liability concerns, and slow university transfer processes) or on the demand side (industry not knowing or trusting that research-lab capacity exists near them)?
- From your conversations with industry partners — particularly in oil and gas, fisheries, ocean tech, and marine sectors that are prominent in Atlantic Canada — how often do procurement teams proactively look to local universities or research institutions as a potential supplier, rather than defaulting to known industrial vendors in Ontario or the US?
- If a platform like Lab2Market could list and verify Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing facilities, including academic labs operating commercially, as certified and available — what would change in your estimation? Would the supply side use it? Would the demand side trust it?

**Why this matters:** Tests CS.2 — whether capable local manufacturing capacity is genuinely idle and disconnected from industrial buyers, and whether the gap is primarily a discovery/trust problem that a verified platform can solve.

### Q3: ElevateIP and Federal Programs — Certification to Commercial Order, How Far Is That Journey?

> **"Canada has built a stack of IP and commercialization programs — ElevateIP through NRC, the Innovation Asset Collective's IP Strategy Program, Springboard's own brokerage function, ACOA funding streams. You're embedded in most of these. The theory is that they help Canadian SMEs build IP positions and arrive at commercial relationships. But I want to understand the gap that these programs don't close: a small Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing facility, maybe doing precision 3D printing or specialized metalwork, completes an ElevateIP program. They've got protected IP, a documented process, maybe a certification. Now what? How far are they from a signed industrial customer contract?"**

**Probes:**
- In your experience, what's the most common stall point for an Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing SME that has gone through an IP or commercialization program? Is it: (a) they still can't get in front of procurement decision-makers, (b) they land meetings but fail on trust/credibility, (c) they have proven capability but no reference customer to show, (d) the procurement process at the target company is too slow to close on, or (e) something else?
- The Springboard Atlantic IP webinar (Libby Carew / Carol Ann Thomas) highlighted a key risk: "Don't build your core IP on someone else's platform. Understand the licensing agreement for SaaS." If a small facility's manufacturing IP is embedded in a third-party software platform or CNC control system, does that create a liability in supplier qualification conversations with large industrial buyers?
- You've flagged that fewer than 10% of Canadian SMEs have a formal IP strategy. For a 3D printing or advanced manufacturing facility trying to serve industrial buyers — is the absence of a formal IP strategy a disqualifier in procurement, or is it only relevant for defense/aerospace/government contracts?
- From your advisory work with start-ups: what is the single most common failure mode when a technically capable Atlantic Canadian manufacturer fails to convert a qualified introduction to an industrial buyer into a commercial contract? Is it a trust problem, a price problem, a process problem, or something structural they can't control?
- What's the fastest you've seen an Atlantic Canadian SME go from "no industrial customer" to "first signed commercial contract" after completing a Springboard or ElevateIP-type intervention? What made it work?

**Why this matters:** Tests VP.1 — whether a platform that surfaces certified, IP-verified facilities to buyers actually closes a real gap, or whether the bottleneck is elsewhere in the commercialization journey and a discovery platform only solves a fraction of the problem.

### Q4: Canada's Defence Industrial Strategy — "Build-Partner-Buy" as a Real Procurement Shift or Political Rhetoric?

> **"You reposted Springboard's detailed breakdown of Canada's new Defence Industrial Strategy — including the 'Build-Partner-Buy' framework, the reworked ITB policy, and the explicit framing of IP as sovereign capability. You wrote that Springboard is 'eager to work with the innovation ecosystem to commercialize IP and turn ideas into sovereign assets.' That's a strong statement. I want to understand how concrete this actually is: has the Defence Industrial Strategy translated into tangible procurement opportunities for Atlantic Canadian SMEs in advanced manufacturing? Or is it still mostly directional language without a changed procurement process on the ground?"**

**Probes:**
- The DIA language is explicit — "directing procurement to domestic firms where we can secure and retain the IP here rather than renting it from abroad." In practice, has the procurement apparatus within DND/PSPC started to behave differently toward Canadian SMEs since this strategy was published? Or are procurement officers still defaulting to the same large primes and US suppliers?
- The strategy mentions expanding NRC support and ElevateIP access for dual-use technology. In Atlantic Canada specifically — given the presence of ocean technology, marine systems, remote sensing, and unmanned systems work — are there advanced manufacturing SMEs who now have a realistic path into defence procurement that they didn't have two years ago?
- The reworked Industrial and Technological Benefits (ITB) policy supposedly shifts from compliance-based offsets to value-added IP creation. In theory, this should benefit smaller Canadian advanced manufacturing suppliers who have proprietary processes. From what you've seen, are large defence primes (Lockheed, SAAB, Rheinmetall, Irving for that matter) actually changing their subcontractor and Canadian SME sourcing behavior as a result, or is the change still years away?
- You've connected Lab2Market and Springboard Atlantic in your repost commentary. If a verified platform existed showing certified Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing facilities — with clear IP ownership, quality certifications, and manufacturing capability data — would that be a usable input for defence prime contractors doing their Canadian content and ITB planning? Who would actually use it at the prime level?
- What's the most important thing an Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing SME needs to do right now — in 2026 — to position itself for defence procurement in the next two to three years? Is it certification, IP protection, revenue proof points, relationships with primes, or something else entirely?

**Why this matters:** Explores whether Canada's policy shift creates real tailwind for Lab2Market's value proposition — specifically whether "Build-Partner-Buy" creates institutional demand for a tool that surfaces certified, IP-documented Canadian advanced manufacturing suppliers for both government and prime contractors.

### Q5: Springboard Atlantic as a Go-To-Market Channel — Supply Side, Demand Side, Both, or Neither?

> **"Let me put a direct question to you. Lab2Market is a platform connecting industrial buyers who need certified advanced manufacturing services — including 3D printing — with verified local facilities. I'm at the stage of figuring out: what's the fastest way to reach both the supply side (facilities with capacity) and the demand side (procurement teams)? Springboard Atlantic has a 19-institution network, active industry partnerships, and relationships with ACOA, government, and start-ups. Is Springboard a realistic go-to-market channel for Lab2Market — and if so, for which side of the market?"**

**Probes:**
- On the supply side: Springboard has relationships with university labs, research SMEs, and innovation spinouts. Is there a mechanism through which Lab2Market could reach Atlantic Canadian advanced manufacturing facilities — academic or commercial — through Springboard's network? Would Springboard be willing to facilitate introductions or co-market to its institutional network?
- On the demand side: Springboard works with industry partners who are looking for technology and innovation from the Atlantic research community. Are those industry partners procurement decision-makers, or are they primarily R&D, innovation, or business development people? Does Springboard have relationships with the procurement and supply chain managers who would actually use Lab2Market?
- Springboard is funded in part through ACOA and federal innovation programs. Is there a funding mechanism — co-commercialization, demonstration project, pilot partnership — through which an alignment between Lab2Market and Springboard could be formalized and resourced?
- You've worked as an entrepreneur and start-up advisor. From your pattern recognition: what's the failure mode when a B2B marketplace tries to use a network-of-networks (associations, post-secondary networks, intermediaries) as their primary go-to-market, rather than direct sales to end customers? What are the signals that a channel strategy is working vs. burning time?
- If you were advising Mahmoud on the 10 most important conversations to have in Atlantic Canada — and nationally — to validate and accelerate Lab2Market's specific model of verified supplier matching for advanced manufacturing, who are the five people or organizations he absolutely needs to sit across from in the next 90 days?

**Why this matters:** Tests CH.1 and CH.2 — whether industry associations and innovation intermediaries like Springboard are effective go-to-market channels for Lab2Market, and surfaces the concrete next steps, referrals, and channel insights that only someone with Niraj's network position can provide.
