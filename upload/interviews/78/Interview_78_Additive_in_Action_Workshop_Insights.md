## STRATEGIC INSIGHT 4: CERTIFICATION IS STILL A MANUAL, RISKY PROCESS

### Aerospace Challenge (Mark Kirby, Tronosjet)

**The Hard Truth About AM Certification:**
Mark Kirby's session revealed that aerospace/space certification for AM parts is:
- **Rigorous** (extensive material testing, process validation)
- **Industry-specific** (varying risk tolerance by sector)
- **Expensive** (certification evidence generation is high-cost)
- **Not standardized** (each part requires custom qualification)

**Key Takeaway:**
> "A key takeaway from this discussion was the difference in risk tolerance between industries. Aerospace and space sectors often operate with varying appetites for risk and innovation depending on the application."

**What This Reveals:**
1. Commercial AM (Michelin's application) = lower certification bar
2. Aerospace AM = extremely high certification bar
3. Defence/Naval (Cameron Munro's world) = different certification bar again
4. **No universal standard yet exists**

**Critical Gap for AddManuChain:**
Your platform must **manage certification complexity across industries**:
- Defence parts (DND, Five Eyes security requirements)
- Maritime parts (Lloyd's, DNV requirements)
- Aerospace parts (FAA, EASA requirements)
- Mining/Industrial (ISO 9001, regional compliance)

**This is NOT straightforward;** it's industry-by-industry governance

## STRATEGIC INSIGHT 5: HANDS-ON EXERCISES REVEAL DECISION-MAKING BARRIERS

### The Drone Wing Exercise (Windows into Adoption Challenges)

**What Happened:**
Workshop participants worked through a **drone wing design exercise** evaluating whether to produce via AM or traditional methods

**Topics That Generated "Extensive Discussion":**
1. **Build Orientation** — How you position the part affects material usage, strength, support structure
2. **Structural Considerations** — Load paths, stress distribution, anisotropy in AM
3. **Support Requirements** — Support structure (scaffolding) needed during build adds cost, post-processing

**The Real Insight:**
These 3 factors are NOT technology questions; they're **design/process questions** that manufacturers don't traditionally ask

**Translation:**
- Traditional manufacturing: Design it, send to vendor, vendor figures out tooling
- AM manufacturing: Design orientation matters, support structure affects cost, structural assumptions change
- **Manufacturers lack mental models for AM decision-making**

**Why This Matters:**
Your platform must do more than match parts to suppliers. It must **educate and guide decision-making** on:
- Optimal build orientation for cost/performance
- Support structure trade-offs
- Design modifications that improve AM feasibility
- Post-processing requirements

## STRATEGIC INSIGHT 6: SELECTAM IS MISSING A "BUSINESS CONTEXT" LAYER

### The Gap Between Technology Feasibility and Business Value

**What SelectAM Does:**
Evaluates technical/cost feasibility of each component individually

**What SelectAM (Probably) Doesn't Do:**
- Prioritize adoption roadmap (which parts first?)
- Link to operational impact (which parts cause most downtime if unavailable?)
- Consider supply chain resilience (which parts are single-sourced?)
- Integrate with inventory systems (which parts are aging/obsolete?)
- Calculate strategic value (which parts support your market differentiation?)

**Bridgewater Workshop Implication:**
The 13 manufacturers see SelectAM as a "technical evaluation tool" but lack **strategic context** for decision-making

**AddManuChain Opportunity (Major):**
Build a **Business Context Layer** on top of SelectAM:
1. **Downtime Cost Analysis:** Which parts, if unavailable, cost most ($/hour of shutdown)?
2. **Supply Chain Health Scoring:** Which parts are at risk (supplier viability, lead time trends, price volatility)?
3. **Strategic Sequencing:** Start with high-impact, low-risk parts; build momentum
4. **ROI Projection:** Show customer path to profitability (not just part-by-part cost)

**This is the missing puzzle piece** that moves from "we could use AM" to "here's our AM adoption roadmap"
