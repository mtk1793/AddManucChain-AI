## KEY INSIGHT #4: CERTIFICATION IS CASE-BY-CASE — SAME PROCESS AS CONVENTIONAL MFG ✅

> **Heather:** *"DNV and Lloyd's treat AM as just another manufacturing procedure. As long as the print facility is qualified for the particular part, the certification process is very much the same — material certificate, machining certificates, final testing certificates, product certificate at the end."*

### DNV vs. Lloyd's Register — Key Difference:

| Body | Approach |
|------|----------|
| **DNV** | Qualifies facilities based on **criticality of parts** (e.g., levels 0–3). Published in their documents. |
| **Lloyd's Register** | Qualifies the **facility's quality management system, machines, and calibrations** regardless of part criticality. Same as qualifying a steel mill for raw materials. |

> **Heather:** *"Lloyd's qualifies the facility for additive manufacturing — it doesn't matter the criticality of the part."*

### Strategic Implication:
- **Atlantic XL (Lloyd's-certified)** is therefore qualified to print parts across all criticality levels — a major competitive advantage
- The certification process is **predictable and documentable** — exactly what AddManuChain's "Certification-as-a-Service" module should automate
- Heather confirmed Aker's internal approach with Lloyd's Register when Mahmoud described the AddManuChain meeting with LR

## KEY INSIGHT #5: OEM IP AND LIABILITY ARE THE TWO STRUCTURAL BARRIERS ⚠️

> **Heather:** *"There's a huge legal IP warranty issue. The OEMs need to be onboard, and it needs to be figured out. The parts they're willing to enter into these agreements for additive manufacturing need to have a business case behind them."*

> **Scott:** *"If someone prints a part, who takes on the liability? Traditionally an OEM would design and manufacture a part, so they'd be responsible for the whole chain. With AM, you're decoupling the design and the manufacturing. Now who owns the liability?"*

- Is it the printer? The OEM? The EPC (Aker)? The operator?

### The Low-Hanging Fruit Solution:

> **Heather:** *"There's a difference with obsolete parts — parts of equipment that OEMs are not supporting anymore. Those are the low-hanging fruit. You sort of skip the OEM step."*

**Translation:** For obsolete parts where the OEM no longer exists or supports the part, you don't need OEM approval. The IP and liability questions are bypassed. This is the beachhead market.

### Strategic Implication:
- **Start with obsolete parts** — OEM barrier doesn't apply
- Build IP/liability framework for OEM-supported parts as Phase 2
- This matches Lee Vessey's Navy insight: non-certified temporary/obsolete parts = immediate opportunity

## KEY INSIGHT #6: IT'S ALWAYS CASE-BY-CASE — PRODUCTION CRITICALITY DRIVES THE DECISION ✅

> **Scott:** *"A screw or a bracket — there could be a situation where we absolutely have to print that bracket from a cost and scheduling perspective. That same bracket might be ordered traditionally if circumstances are slightly different. It all comes down to criticality — safety criticality, but also production criticality. If it has production criticality, there's an extreme cost savings that typically pushes the part over the edge for AM."*

### Real Examples Scott Shared:
- ✅ **Can openers offshore** — printed constantly. Non-critical, high use, easy.
- ✅ **Scaffolding components** — printed regularly. Low safety implication, commercial case exists.
- ⚠️ **Impeller for a pump** — could be printed, but you still have to bring the pump onshore to replace it. What is the real saving?

> **Scott:** *"It's something that's really cool. But does it really save time and cost over traditional manufacturing?"*

### Strategic Implication:
- AddManuChain's AI must include a **production criticality calculator** — not just safety criticality
- The ROI case must answer: "If this part fails, what does the cascade shutdown cost per hour?"
- This aligns directly with Douglas (Equinor) data: 1-2% capex protects 98% of production value

## KEY INSIGHT #7: DIGITAL INVENTORY DEMAND IS REAL — PLATFORM GAP EXISTS IN CANADA ✅

> **Scott:** *"It sounds like that's what we use FieldNode for at the moment. It just needs to be established better in Canada — whether it's FieldNode or some other platform."*

> **Scott:** *"As the ecosystem develops and you see these types of platforms develop — if you establish something like that in Canada, then you're going to end up with a digital inventory that may or may not get used based on the situation."*

### Strategic Implication:
- Scott explicitly called out the **Canada gap** — there is no well-established AM digital inventory platform in Canada
- He did not dismiss AddManuChain — he framed it as the Canadian version of what FieldNode is doing globally
- The question is not "do we need this?" but "will it get used enough to justify the inventory build-up?"
