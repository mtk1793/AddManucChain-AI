## WHAT HE CONFIRMED (Pain Validation)

### 1. The $15 Part / $5,000 Shipping Problem
The most vivid example he gave: a **plastic generator component worth ~$15** that cost **$5,000 to ship** because of the remoteness of his site. This is the perfect AddManuChain case study:
- Intrinsic part value: negligible
- Logistics cost: 333× part value
- AM would solve this entirely — print locally, no freight

**Implication:** His most pressing AM opportunity is not complex metal components. It's **small, inexpensive polymer parts** where the shipping and lead-time cost dominates. Emergency Response + AM Feasibility Triage directly addresses this.

### 2. Min/Max Inventory System — But It Has Limits
Mike uses a **min/max inventory system calibrated to lead times** — when stock drops to the minimum, a reorder is triggered. He maintains two storage points:
- On-site inventory (fast-access critical parts)
- Offsite warehouse (secondary buffer)

**The gap:** This system works when lead times are predictable. It breaks when:
- A part is discontinued / obsolete (OEM no longer makes it)
- Lead time spikes unexpectedly
- A remote site is 6 months from reliable supply

His system is an operational workaround for a structural problem. AddManuChain doesn't compete with his inventory system — it **extends its reach** to parts his system can't cover.

### 3. Downtime Cost Is Real But Hard to Quantify for Outsiders
Mike confirmed downtime costs are severe — **revenue loss + active expenditure simultaneously** — but said he couldn't give a precise number in the interview context. This is typical: maintenance superintendents know downtime hurts but the exact $/hr figure lives with the finance team.

**Implication:** Don't lead with a downtime cost figure when approaching his level. Instead, use the *"revenue stops + costs continue"* framing he used — it resonates. Push the exact number ask to CFO/Finance in follow-up.

### 4. Remote Site Validation — "6 Months From Supply"
Mike explicitly mentioned working at sites **6 months from reliable supply chain access**. At those locations, the gap between a part failure and a part in hand can be weeks to months. He said AM would give operations teams *"more peace of mind"* — unprompted, genuine.

This validates the **Emergency Response** use case and the **on-demand print network** concept directly from someone who has lived the problem across multiple continents.

### 5. Redundancy-First Mindset — Now That Revenue Is Flowing
Since Greenstone is generating revenue (relatively new mine, now past startup phase), Mike is actively **building redundancies and bypasses into their processes**. This is a key signal:
- He is in capital allocation mode
- He is open to investments that reduce operational risk
- He is thinking about spare parts strategy with a longer horizon

This is the right moment to engage — not during startup chaos, but when they have budget and a reliability mandate.

## STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS FOR ADDMANUCHAIN

### 1. Mining Segment Validation Is Strong
Mike is the 3rd+ mining maintenance expert to validate the core pain (alongside earlier interviews with JP and others). The pattern is consistent:
- Remote sites + high logistics costs = AM use case is obvious to them
- Inventory system limitations are real and acknowledged
- AM technology openness is higher than expected — most have already touched some form of reverse engineering or digital manufacturing

### 2. Lead With Polymer/Consumable Parts, Not Metal
Across mining interviews, the **first pilot opportunity is always small polymer and consumable parts**, not complex metal alloys. The value proposition is clearest here:
- No certification complexity (non-structural)
- Immediate cost savings (freight vs. print)
- Fast proof of concept — part printed in hours, not months of qualification

**Pitch sequence:** Start with caps/seals/polymer components → prove value → expand to metal structural parts → introduce certification and DRM layers.

### 3. Reverse Engineering Capability Is a Bridge
Mike already has 3D scanning capability. Other mining interviews (JP) confirmed similar. This means:
- AddManuChain doesn't need to sell the scanning step
- The platform should explicitly support **"bring your own scan" → upload to cooperative → qualify → print** workflow
- This is a feature worth adding to the demo: "Upload your reverse-engineered geometry directly to the Digital Cooperative"

### 4. Greenstone Is a Viable Pilot Candidate
- Remote location (Geraldton, Northern Ontario) validates the logistics argument
- Mine is revenue-generating and in redundancy-investment mode — budget exists
- Mike gave permission for follow-up — he is open
- 3D scanning already in use — low onboarding friction

**Pilot proposal: start with 3–5 polymer consumables, print locally through a vetted Ontario AM facility, measure lead time and cost savings vs. traditional procurement.**
