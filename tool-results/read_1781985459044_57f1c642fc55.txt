    12→## 1. CENTRALIZED VS. DECENTRALIZED: THE GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORK
    13→
    14→**Insight:** Sebastian provided a precise framework for what MUST remain centralized vs. what CAN be distributed. This is the most actionable strategic guidance from any interviewee to date.
    15→
    16→### What MUST Be Centralized (Non-Negotiable):
    17→
    18→1. **Machine Calibration Standards**
    19→   - All printing machines must operate identically
    20→   - Calibration protocols must be identical
    21→   - Maintenance standards must be identical
    22→   - Sebastian: "You need to make sure that you will start with the basics and calibrate the machines against the same standards, maintain the machines against the same standards. That's absolutely non-negotiable."
    23→
    24→2. **Powder/Feedstock Specifications**
    25→   - Material composition must be standardized
    26→   - Quality control on feedstock is non-negotiable
    27→   - Traceability required (batch tracking)
    28→   - Sebastian: "Off Powders and feedstock to the same specification absolutely"
    29→
    30→3. **Process Parameters**
    31→   - Temperature profiles must match
    32→   - Cooling parameters must match
    33→   - Post-print treatment protocols must match
    34→   - Goal: "Get the same material out of the printer"
    35→
    36→4. **Design Standards**
    37→   - Part design must be centralized (OEM responsibility)
    38→   - Performance specifications must be centralized
    39→   - Certification requirements must be centralized
    40→   - Critical for liability/warranty protection
    41→
    42→### What CAN Be Decentralized (Flexible):
    43→
    44→1. **Post-Processing Chain**
    45→   - Can be in-house or outsourced
    46→   - Can vary by location (different operational setups)
    47→   - Less critical than printing itself
    48→   - Sebastian: "What can be localized is really how you set up your post-processing chain. Then is it inside or outside of your company? So outsourcing a topic. So that's it's more freedom I guess."
    49→
    50→2. **Powder Handling/Preparation**
    51→   - How to mix, prepare, handle powder can vary
    52→   - Must meet specifications, but methods can differ
    53→   - Sebastian: "The powder itself that goes again and again into the process needs to fulfill certain requirements. But how you deal with mixing powder and whatnot that can be different in different sites."
    54→
    55→3. **Operational Setup**
    56→   - How facilities are organized locally
    57→   - Staffing models (in-house vs. vendor)
    58→   - Sebastian: "For example, something that you can adapt to the operational setup"
    59→
    60→### Strategic Implication for AddManuChain:
    61→
    62→This framework means **distributed manufacturing is possible IF you respect the hierarchy**:
    63→
    64→**Tier 1 - Non-Negotiable (Must be controlled by you):**
    65→- Machine specification/calibration protocols
    66→- Feedstock sourcing/quality control
    67→- Process parameter standardization
    68→- Design control (you provide certified designs; partners execute)
    69→
    70→**Tier 2 - Negotiable (Can be distributed to regional partners):**
    71→- Post-processing methods
    72→- Facility operations
    73→- Local staffing/logistics
    74→- Qualification/regulatory navigation
    75→
    76→**Tier 3 - Partnership Model (Can be external vendors):**
    77→- Partners source machines (to your spec)
    78→- Partners source feedstock (to your spec)
    79→- Partners execute processes (to your spec)
    80→- Partners handle post-processing (methods can vary)
    81→- Partners manage regulatory compliance (within your standards)
    82→
    83→**Recommendation:** Position AddManuChain as "Tier 1 Controller + Tier 2/3 Enabler" - you provide standards and design; regional partners execute under your governance.

    87→## 2. OEM BUSINESS MODEL: THREAT VS. OPPORTUNITY ANALYSIS
    88→
    89→**Insight:** Sebastian was explicit about viewing third-party manufacturing as **primarily a threat**, not an opportunity. This is crucial for partnership strategy.
    90→
    91→### Why OEMs See Distributed Manufacturing as Threat:
    92→
    93→1. **Service Revenue Erosion**
    94→   - Historically, OEMs earn significant margins on spare parts
    95→   - If third parties can produce parts, customers become independent
    96→   - Sebastian: "From our perspective as an OEM, we see this more as a threat. There an opportunity. Our customers that have engines in the fields, they probably see that more as an opportunity to be a bit more independent from us."
    97→
    98→2. **Customer Control Loss**
    99→   - OEMs currently control the maintenance relationship via parts scarcity
   100→   - Third-party manufacturing allows customers to choose vendors
   101→   - Reduces long-term contract leverage
   102→
   103→3. **Quality/Warranty Risk**
   104→   - If non-OEM parts fail, OEM may still bear liability
   105→   - Quality control becomes indirect (can't inspect production)
   106→   - Warranty claims become unpredictable
   107→   - Sebastian: "It's simply a risk topic"
   108→
   109→4. **Margin Cannibalization**
   110→   - For "mature frames" (older turbines), third parties may undercut OEM pricing
   111→   - Sebastian: "For for mature frames, we have this situation that there are so parties that offers capability. Now, so that's cannibalizing the market."
   112→
   113→### Why OEMs MIGHT Accept It (Opportunity):
   114→
   115→1. **Spare Parts Availability = Contract Driver**
   116→   - Fast parts availability can command premium pricing
   117→   - Sebastian: "If you come up with spare parts in no time. So that is a business case in itself."
   118→   - If third parties accelerate availability, customers may accept higher service fees
   119→
   120→2. **Supply Chain Resilience (Real Constraint)**
   121→   - OEMs face physical scaling limits (can't expand internal capacity infinitely)
   122→   - External vendors reduce risk of over-capacity investment
   123→   - Sebastian: "External growth will not solve our next 4 years challenge, so we need to onboard external vendors for part production"
   124→
   125→3. **New Design/Material Challenges**
   126→   - Complex new designs (like hydrogen combustion) may require fresh expertise
   127→   - Could be opportunity to find specialized partners
   128→   - Sebastian: "But if you have your basics right then there is nothing that speaks against producing parts at more than one location"
   129→
   130→### Strategic Implication:
   131→
   132→**Be Clear About Your Role:**
   133→- NOT: "We'll replace OEM supply chains"
   134→- YES: "We'll handle overflow demand + obsolete parts + customer-preferred on-site production"
   135→
   136→**Positioning Framework:**
   137→- Frame as **demand fulfillment partner**, not competitor
   138→- Handle:
   139→  - Peak demand periods (OEM can't keep up)
   140→  - Obsolete parts (OEM has discontinued)
   141→  - On-site production (customer preference)
   142→  - Aged fleet support (low-margin for OEM)
   143→- Strictly NOT:
   144→  - New, IP-critical designs
   145→  - Parts under active warranty
   146→  - Core customer relationships

   229→## 5. CYBERSECURITY: THE UNDERESTIMATED CRITICAL PATH
   230→
   231→**Insight:** Sebastian emphasized cybersecurity **multiple times** and was explicit about it being underestimated by potential partners: "Cybersecurity is a very important thing...So don't underestimate this issue about your business model and your business idea."
   232→
   233→### Why Cybersecurity Matters:
   234→
   235→1. **IP Protection Vehicle**
   236→   - Shared digital platform = IP exposure risk
   237→   - CAD designs, process parameters, materials specs all vulnerable
   238→   - One compromised vendor = all IP exposed
   239→   - Sebastian: "We usually do is we go through a due diligence with our external vendors regarding cybersecurity"
   240→
   241→2. **Regulatory/Compliance Risk**
   242→   - Some designs may be ITARS-controlled (export regulations)
   243→   - Some processes may be classified (defence applications)
   244→   - Cybersecurity breach = regulatory violation + legal liability
   245→
   246→3. **Operational Continuity Risk**
   247→   - If platform is hacked, all partner production could be disrupted
   248→   - Ransomware vulnerability
   249→   - Supply chain attack surface
   250→
   251→### Current Practice at Siemens:
   252→
   253→1. **Controlled Digital Spaces**
   254→   - Separate collaboration platform for vendor access
   255→   - Not an open API; controlled/gated access
   256→   - Vendors can "pull information" but not freely browse
   257→   - Sebastian: "We run a collaboration platform where you can put it's a controlled space, digital space, where you can put your models and all the specifications that you need to produce the part and then our vendor can pull the information from there."
   258→
   259→2. **Due Diligence Process**
   260→   - Formal cybersecurity assessment of each vendor
   261→   - Standards must be maintained
   262→   - Ongoing compliance monitoring
   263→
   264→### Strategic Implication:
   265→
   266→**Don't position as "open data platform."** Position as **"controlled governance platform with security standards."**
   267→
   268→**Requirements for OEM Adoption:**
   269→- Cybersecurity certification (ISO 27001 minimum; likely need SOC 2 Type II)
   270→- Regular penetration testing / vulnerability assessments
   271→- Vendor due diligence process (not just self-attestation)
   272→- Data encryption (both transit and at rest)
   273→- Access logging / audit trails
   274→- Compliance with ITARS (if applicable)
   275→- DND security clearance (if defence applications)
   276→
   277→**Recommendation:**
   278→- Build cybersecurity as **core platform feature**, not afterthought
   279→- Get third-party security audit early (not after launch)
   280→- Engage OEM compliance/security teams early in design
   281→- Make this a competitive advantage, not compliance burden
